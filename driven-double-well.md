#Driven Double-Well Circuit for Audio-Rate Analog Synthesis

This project explores audio-rate analog synthesis by directly implementing a nonlinear dynamical system in hardware.

The circuit is a physical analog realization of a driven double-well system related to the Duffing equation. Rather than starting from a conventional oscillator waveform and shaping it afterward, the circuit solves the system continuously in real time using integrators, summers, transconductance stages, and analog multipliers.

In this sense, the circuit is both a sound source and a small analog computer.

This sign structure is important. The negative linear term and positive cubic term create a double-well potential, in contrast to the single-well behavior of the harmonic oscillator.

Analog computing approach

The general method is to rearrange the differential equation in terms of the lowest-order derivative to be solved directly, then realize the system with cascaded integrator stages.

For a second-order equation such as

$$x^{\prime\prime} + b x^\prime - x + x^3 = E(t)$$

we rearrange to isolate the highest derivative:

$$x^{\prime\prime} = -b x^\prime + x - x^3 + E(t)$$

This is then split into a pair of first-order state equations. For example, defining

$$x^\prime = y$$

gives

$$x^\prime = y$$
$$y^\prime = -b y + x - x^3 + E(t)$$

Why this is different from conventional subtractive synthesis

Most analog synthesizers are built around a stable oscillator-plus-filter model. This circuit belongs to a different category.

Here, the sound is generated by the real-time solution of a nonlinear differential equation. Parameters such as:

damping
drive level
drive frequency

do not simply shape tone after the fact; they alter the qualitative behavior of the system itself.

Depending on parameter settings, the circuit can exhibit:

motion confined to one well
periodic transitions between wells
irregular switching
chaotic behavior

The output is therefore better understood as the observed behavior of a dynamical system than as a conventional oscillator waveform.

Notes on implementation

A few practical observations from the current build:

The double-well behavior depends critically on the sign of the restoring-force terms.
The linear and cubic terms must resolve to the equivalent of:
-x + x^3

in the normalized equation.

The damping term acts independently of the nonlinear restoring block and sets the effective coefficient of x
′
.
In multiplier-based implementations, intermediate squaring stages may exhibit DC components, but the final cubic term is odd and ideally does not introduce a net offset by itself.
In practice, offsets and asymmetries can still arise from multiplier feedthrough, op-amp offset, OTA mismatch, and grounding/layout effects.

Motivation

The broader aim of this work is to explore analog synthesis as a subset of analog computation.

Conventional oscillator/filter architectures remain important, but they do not exhaust the range of continuous-time systems that can operate as sound-generating circuits. Nonlinear state-space circuits such as the driven double-well system suggest a wider design space for analog musical instruments.
